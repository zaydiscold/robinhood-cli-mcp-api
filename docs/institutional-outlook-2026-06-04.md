## 1. Current-regime synthesis (one paragraph)

As of mid-2026 the major houses share a recognizable regime read: a **non-recessionary, AI-capex-led expansion** in which a historic hyperscaler buildout (estimates clustered at **~$800B of AI capex in 2026 rising toward ~$1T+ by 2027–28**) is large enough to move the macro, not just tech earnings — even as it front-loads investment, leverages up the financial system, and concentrates index returns in a handful of names. The consensus macro backdrop is **sturdy US growth (~2.0–2.6% real), sticky-but-tolerable inflation (running hot, ~3.8% headline in Apr-2026 on an energy/Iran shock), and a Fed on hold (3.50–3.75% as of the Apr-2026 meeting, few or no cuts priced, leadership transitioning from Powell to Warsh)**. The sharpest divergence is **valuation vs. momentum**: the sell-side strategists (Goldman, Morgan Stanley) stay outright bullish on US equities into 2026–27 on earnings, while the long-horizon CMA shops (Vanguard, GMO/State Street, Research Affiliates, and to a lesser degree JPM/Fidelity) warn that rich US starting valuations imply **low single-digit 10-yr US equity returns**, favoring **high-quality bonds, US value, and non-US/EM equities** over the next decade. Nearly everyone now treats **fixed income as structurally re-attractive** (yields reset higher), flags **stock-bond correlation drifting positive** (diversification is harder), and names the same structural "mega forces": **AI/datacenter capex, geopolitical fragmentation / economic nationalism / reshoring, fiscal-debt overhang, demographics/labor constraints, and the energy/power transition.**

## 3. Consensus vs. divergence

**Where the houses AGREE (the consensus regime view):**
- **AI capex is a macro force, not just a tech trade.** All of BlackRock, Vanguard, JPM, Goldman, Morgan Stanley, UBS, BNY anchor on the ~$800B-2026 → ~$1T+-2027/28 hyperscaler buildout as the dominant earnings/growth driver.
- **No recession in the base case.** Sturdy US growth (~2.0–2.6%), labor cooling but stabilizing, soft-ish landing.
- **Inflation is sticky and the Fed is on hold / cutting slowly.** Energy (Iran/Middle East) is the shared upside-inflation risk; few cuts priced.
- **Fixed income is structurally re-attractive.** The yield reset means bonds carry real return again; HQ bonds are the most-favored diversifier almost everywhere (Vanguard #1, JPM, PIMCO, UBS).
- **Diversification is harder.** Stock-bond correlation drifting positive (JPM explicit), and index concentration means passive US exposure is "an active bet" (BlackRock "diversification mirage").
- **Same five mega forces** (see §4).

**Where they DISAGREE (the notable contrarian/divergent calls):**
- **Valuation bears vs. momentum bulls — the central split.** Long-horizon CMA shops say **US equities deliver only ~4–6% over 10–20 yrs** (Vanguard 4–5%, Fidelity 5.8% nominal, GMO/State Street implying equities < bonds near-term, Research Affiliates historically lowest). The sell-side strategists (**Goldman, Morgan Stanley**) are near-term **outright bullish** (S&P 8,000+ into 2026, 8,300 mid-2027 on 20%+ EPS growth). These aren't contradictory in horizon (1-yr target vs 10-yr CMA) but they *frame risk oppositely.*
- **US vs. ex-US.** Vanguard, Fidelity, BNY, JPM lean toward **non-US developed + EM and US value** outperforming over the decade; BlackRock stays **OW US** (plus Japan) tactically. Genuine disagreement on where the next decade's equity return lives.
- **Credit.** PIMCO and UBS are **cautious on tight-spread corporate credit** (PIMCO prefers asset-based finance); Morgan Stanley explicitly expects **credit to lag** on AI-debt issuance — a more defensive credit read than the equity bullishness implies.
- **Duration.** PIMCO **OW 5–10yr / UW 30yr** (re-steepening); BlackRock **UW long-term Treasuries**. Broad agreement to avoid the long end, but for different stated reasons (term premium vs. fiscal/leverage).
- **Bonds-beat-stocks?** PIMCO (and the State Street averaged 12-mo forecast) say fixed income can **outperform equities** near-term — a direct counter to the equity-strategist bull case.

## 4. The structural "mega forces" — cross-firm

These recur across nearly every house and are the durable, slow-moving themes an agent should treat as the *backdrop* for sector/ticker attention:

1. **AI / datacenter / power capex.** The dominant theme everywhere. Front-loaded investment, back-loaded revenue (BlackRock "leveraging up"); ~half of 2026 S&P earnings growth (Goldman); spans semis, hyperscalers, power/utilities, grid, cooling, infrastructure. Bubble-risk caveat (UBS, Vanguard "exuberance").
2. **Geopolitical fragmentation / economic nationalism / reshoring.** JPM's headline ("new era of economic nationalism"); BlackRock's "geopolitical fragmentation"; PIMCO's "Fragmentation Era." Tariffs, supply-chain reshoring, defense/industrials, friend-shoring.
3. **Fiscal & debt overhang.** Highly indebted governments + a more levered system (BlackRock); fiscal activism (JPM); the One Big Beautiful Bill Act tailwind (Vanguard); supports the UW-long-duration / higher-term-premium view.
4. **Demographics & labor constraints.** JPM "labor constraints," BlackRock demographics — a structural drag on long-run growth and an inflation floor.
5. **Energy / low-carbon transition + energy security.** BlackRock "low-carbon transition"; the acute 2026 wrinkle is the **Middle East/Iran energy supply shock** re-accelerating inflation — the shared near-term risk. Power demand from AI links this directly to mega force #1.

## 5. How an agent should USE this layer

This doc is the **slow, high-authority macro/thesis layer.** Its job is to *frame attention*, never to trigger an order. Concretely:

- **It is the slowest tier in the signal stack.** Order of speed: **X/Twitter (fastest pulse) → Reddit → RH `midlands/news|ratings|tags` (slow broker-native confirmer) → these institutional outlooks (slowest, highest-authority, regime-level).** Use it to set the *frame* the fast signals are interpreted against — e.g. if the X pulse spikes on a power-grid name, this layer tells you that maps onto a cross-firm mega force (AI capex → power), which *raises* the prior that it's signal not noise. The fast layers still lead; this layer contextualizes.
- **It frames, never dictates, ticker/sector attention.** Use the consensus mega forces (§4) to decide *where to point the universe* — semis/hyperscalers/power for AI capex, defense/industrials for fragmentation/reshoring, quality bonds / US value / non-US for the CMA-bear tilt. It does **not** select a contract, set a strike, or imply a position. It is a prior, not a trade.
- **Feed it into Ball Knowledge as regime context, not as alpha.** When a `ball-knowledge.md` entry (a ticker, sector, or `@handle`) aligns with a cross-firm mega force, say so plainly ("your semis note maps onto the AI-capex mega force every major house is anchored on; that's regime tailwind, not confirmation of this specific thesis"). When an entry runs *against* consensus (e.g. an aggressively bullish small-cap long-US-growth bet vs. the CMA-bear "low US returns" view), surface the divergence neutrally — it's the operator's call, and contrarian-vs-consensus is itself useful framing.
- **Use it to weight, not gate.** A consensus tailwind slightly raises the prior that a fast signal is real; a consensus headwind is a "be aware the house view disagrees" flag — not a veto. Never let this layer override live market data, brokerage order history, or explicit user intent (same rule as Ball Knowledge: context, not authority, not permission).
- **Hold both sides of the central split.** Present the **momentum/bull** read (Goldman/MS earnings case) *and* the **valuation/CMA-bear** read (Vanguard/Fidelity/GMO low-10yr-return case) when framing US equity exposure — they answer different horizons. Don't collapse them into one "the market thinks X."
- **Cite the firm + as-of date when this layer shapes an answer** ("per JPM's 2026 LTCMA, Oct-2025, US large-cap 10-yr expected ≈6.7% — a low-return decade prior"), the same way SKILL.md requires naming when Ball Knowledge shaped a call.
